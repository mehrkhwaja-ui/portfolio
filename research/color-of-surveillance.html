The Color of Surveillance

In May 2025, I wondered what drives surveillance in NYC neighborhoods, and made a map to find out. Exploring the relationship between race, protest, and facial recognition surveillance in New York City.

GIS Mapping Geospatial Analysis Data Visualization Cartography

Context

In 2024, Amnesty International mapped every surveillance camera across NYC's 45,000 intersections and found that higher camera concentrations correlated with more stop-and-frisk incidents against non-white residents. Their research documented the consequences of surveillance.

I wanted to investigate the cause: were the cameras themselves being placed based on race, or was something else at play?

Method

I used Amnesty's dataset alongside NYU Furman Center's demographic data to map the dominant ethnic groups per neighborhood and compare them against the distribution of Argus facial recognition cameras. To account for geographic differences, I measured camera density per hectare rather than raw totals — a larger district would naturally have more cameras than a smaller one, so proportional analysis was critical.

Findings

What I found was unexpected. The areas with the highest camera density weren't necessarily the most racially diverse neighborhoods. Instead, they clustered in the West Village and Midtown — areas that have seen the highest concentration of protests over the past few years, particularly Pro-Palestine and Black Lives Matter demonstrations. The surveillance infrastructure, it turns out, isn't just watching people — it's watching dissent.
